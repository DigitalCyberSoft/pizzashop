📋 For the grown-ups

👩‍🏫 For Parents & Teachers

Pizza Palace is a reading-comprehension game disguised as a cooking game. Every order is a short piece of English the child reads, understands, and acts on. The difficulty is in the language, not the tapping: orders grow from "a whole pizza with ham" to multi-sentence instructions with recipes, negations, conditionals, and spatial rules like "the two slices directly across from each other."

What it teaches

  • Reading carefully and following multi-step instructions in order.
  • Spatial and quantity language: half, quarter, every other, next to, opposite, except.
  • Ignoring distractions: chatter and red-herring lines that must be read past, not followed.
  • Memory and inference: recalling a named recipe, or working out that "me, my wife and two kids" means four quarters.

The concepts, with examples

Each idea below is a real teaching target, and every bold word is tappable inside the game: the child taps it and a little pizza pops up to show what it means. The same cards live on a single review page you can open at game.html#glossary.

Fractions and sharing — the pizza is 8 slices, so it cuts cleanly into the maths. Why: it builds early fraction sense (half, quarter, eighth) and turns a worded situation into a division the child can act out.

  • Whole = all 8 slices. "Make a whole pizza with ham."
  • Half = 4 of 8 = 1/2. "Ham on one half, mushroom on the other."
  • Quarter = 2 of 8 = 1/4. "Pepperoni on the top-left quarter."
  • Slice = 1 of 8 = 1/8. "Just one slice of pineapple."
  • Sharing turns words into a fraction. "For me, my wife and our two kids" → four quarters.
  • Uneven sharing mixes division with "more than". "Split my other half between Sam and Joey, but Sam eats more" → Sam gets 3 slices, Joey 1.

Position and shape — where things sit, and how slices relate. Why: it trains spatial vocabulary and orientation-independent thinking — orders use relations (opposite, next to) not fixed directions, so the child reasons about the shape, not a memorised side.

  • Next to / touching: two slices that share an edge.
  • Opposite: straight across the pizza (slice 1 and slice 5).
  • Diagonal: two quarters corner-to-corner.
  • Every other: skip one each time, all the way round.
  • Three in a row: three touching slices in a line.

Number and comparison — counting and inequalities. Why: it puts "how many" into words — exact counts, greater-than/less-than, and "at least one" — which is where reading and arithmetic meet.

  • Exactly N: "exactly one fruit on every slice" — no more, no fewer.
  • More than / fewer than: "more than two meats" means 3, 4, 5… (greater-than); "fewer than five" means 4 or below.
  • At least one: "at least one meat on every slice" — one is fine, more is fine.

Sets and categories — which toppings belong together, including overlaps. Why: it teaches classification and set membership — grouping things, and the idea that one thing can belong to two groups at once (the basis of Venn diagrams later).

  • Meat, vegetable, fruit, silly topping: the four food groups orders draw on.
  • Belonging to two groups: broccoli is a vegetable and a silly topping; banana is a fruit and silly.
  • Intersection: the part in both groups at once — spatial ("slices in both the top half and the right half") and by category ("a topping that is both a fruit AND silly" → banana or raisins).
  • Shared property: group by a trait they share — "one green topping on every slice."
  • Not touching: keep two groups apart with a gap — "the meat must never touch the veg."

Logic and language — the trickier reading at the top levels. Why: this is the heart of comprehension — following a reference back, evaluating a condition, ruling options out, and inferring an unstated rule. Each is a step beyond reading the words literally.

  • Except / without: do it all, leave one part out. "Cheese everywhere except the bottom-right quarter."
  • Coverage with an exception: a positive rule plus a small "not". "Cover it all in banana, but one slice should NOT have banana."
  • Riddle: name a topping by a clue, not its word. "It is a round red circle of spicy meat" → pepperoni.
  • Pronouns (it, the other one): track what a word points back to. "Put ham on one half. Now put it on the other half too."
  • Either / or, not both: choose exactly one of two options. "Either ham or pineapple — you pick one."
  • Conditional: check whether something is true, then act. "Is pepperoni a meat? It is! So cover the whole pizza in it."
  • Deduction by elimination: rule out groups to find what is left. "Not a meat, not a vegetable, not a fruit" → a silly topping.
  • Inference: work out an unstated rule from a fact. "My brother can't eat meat" → put no meat on it.
  • Named pizza (recipe): a name standing for a fixed recipe. "A Hawaiian" = ham + pineapple, built from raw ingredients from memory.
  • Base: the sauce laid first (tomato, cheese or BBQ) before any topping.
  • Red herrings & self-correction: chatter to ignore, and orders that change their mind mid-sentence ("actually, scrap that…").

Rules and instruction words — precise direction-following (the kind used in recipes, exams and assembly steps). Why: children are constantly told to do things with words of different force. Knowing that must ≠ may ≠ must not is the difference between following instructions exactly and roughly.

  • Must / shall / required: you have to do it. "You MUST put pepperoni on one half."
  • May / optional / should / recommended: your choice — doing it and skipping it are both fine.
  • Must not / shall not: never do it. "You MUST NOT use any olives." The game accepts the required parts, allows the optional ones either way, and marks the forbidden ones wrong.

Under the hood: this is early Boolean logic Why: several of the constructs above are the exact building blocks of Boolean algebra — the binary logic that all computer arithmetic is built from. It is genuine computational thinking, dressed up as pizza, years before a child meets a truth table.

  • AND: "both ham AND mushroom on every slice," or a topping that is "a fruit AND silly" (the intersection of two sets).
  • OR: "either ham or pineapple" — at least one of the choices is true.
  • NOT: "no meat," "must not," "one slice should not have banana."
  • Exclusive OR (XOR): "one or the other, but not both."
  • True / false: "is pepperoni a meat? Yes" — evaluating a condition before acting on it.

"Vegetarian" is taught as a word (a pizza with no meat) rather than a graded rule: the game checks the slices, not the label.

Ages & levels

Twenty levels span roughly ages 4 to 12. The level adapts automatically: fast, accurate pizzas raise it; mistakes or running out of time lower it, so the game settles where the child is challenged but not stuck. Nothing to configure.

The money is the gentle stakes

The shop starts with $20; each pizza costs $3 to make and a correct one sells for $5 (a $2 profit), plus a $1 tip for being quick and accurate. It teaches that reading carefully has a payoff, with no harsh sting beyond reopening the shop.
