👩‍🏫 Parents & Teachers Guide

Pizza Palace is a reading-comprehension game disguised as a cooking game. Every order is a short piece of English a child reads, understands, and acts on. The difficulty lives in the language, not the tapping.

How it works in one minute

A customer asks for a pizza in words. The child reads the order, lays a base, taps on toppings to build it, and presses Box it!. Get it right and the customer pays; get it wrong and the game shows what the order actually meant, in plain English, then moves on.

  • It adapts by itself. Fast, accurate pizzas raise the level; mistakes or running out of time lower it. The game settles where the child is challenged but not stuck. Nothing to configure.
  • Gentle stakes. The shop starts with $20. Each pizza costs $3 to make and a correct one sells for $5 (a $2 profit), plus a $1 tip for being quick and accurate. Reading carefully has a visible payoff, with no harsh sting beyond reopening the shop.
  • Tappable words. Hard words in every order are underlined. Tapping one opens a little pizza that shows what it means, so the game teaches its own vocabulary as it goes.

What success — and a miss — look like

Get it right and the customer celebrates and pays (with a bonus for being quick). Get it wrong and the pizza is shown back against what was actually asked, with the idea explained in plain English. Either way, the child learns something.

Ages 4–6 · Levels 1–5

First words and first fractions

The youngest players meet one idea at a time. Orders are a single short sentence, with one or two toppings, and the pizza is split no finer than halves. This is where a child learns that words map to actions and that a round thing can be cut into equal parts.

Whole: "A whole pizza on a tomato base, covered all over in pineapple."

Halves: "Tomato base all over, then one half ham and the other half mushroom."

Two pizzas (from level 3): "Two pizzas, please! Make one an all-cheese pizza, and the other all pepperoni."

Ages 6–9 · Levels 6–12

Quarters, positions and groups

Now the sentences get longer and the pizza divides into quarters and single slices. Orders start using relationships (next to, opposite, every other) and food categories (meat, vegetable, fruit, silly). The child has to hold several facts in mind and decide where things go.

Quarters & single slicesOpposite / next to / every other Counting (exactly N)Food categoriesTwo toppings on a sliceCounts across two pizzas

Quarters: "A BBQ base, with ham on just one quarter and sausage on the other three quarters."

Every other: "A cheese base all over, then fish heads on every other slice, all the way around."

Category, your choice: "A tomato base, and make it SILLY! Every slice gets exactly one silly topping."

Two-pizza counts (from level 10): "For my two pizzas: 4 slices of pepperoni, the rest cheese." (4 + 12 = 16)

Ages 9–12 · Levels 13–25

Logic, inference and precise instructions

The top levels are where reading becomes reasoning. Orders carry conditions, negations, choices, named recipes, distractor chatter to ignore, and rules with precise force. Several are, underneath the pizza, genuine Boolean logic (see below). The child has to parse, infer, and act exactly.

If / then & true / falseEither-or, not bothDeduction by elimination Inference (no meat → a veg)Must / may / must notUneven sharingFractions over 16 (big fractions)

Big fractions (from level 15): "For my two pizzas: five eighths cheese, three eighths Meat Feast." (10 + 6 = 16; five eighths is more than a whole pizza)

Rules (must / may / must not): "Order rules! On a tomato base: you SHALL put pepper on one half. You SHOULD add chilli to the other half. And you SHALL NOT use any extra cheese."

Uneven share: "Give my other half to my two kids: Joey wants Brussels sprout, Ava wants sweetcorn, but Joey eats MORE than Ava, so Joey gets 3 slices and Ava just 1."

Deduction: "Mystery topping! It is NOT a meat, NOT a vegetable and NOT a fruit. Work out what is left, and put one on every slice."

Every concept, with examples

Each idea below is a real teaching target, and every bold word is tappable inside the game. The same cards live on a single review page at game.html#glossary.

Fractions and sharing Why: builds fraction sense from halves and quarters up to eighths and fractions bigger than a whole, and turns a worded situation into a division the child can act out.

  • Whole = all 8 slices (8/8).
  • Half = 4 of 8 = 1/2.
  • Quarter = 2 of 8 = 1/4.
  • Slice = 1 of 8 = 1/8.
  • Sharing turns words into a fraction: "for me, my wife and our two kids" → four quarters.
  • Uneven sharing mixes division with "more than": "split my other half, but Sam eats more" → Sam 3 slices, Joey 1.
  • Two pizzas (from level 3) = 16 slices. A simple two-pizza order is the gentle start — "one cheese pizza and one Hawaiian" — graded a pizza at a time.
  • Counts over 16 (from level 10): one order, both pizzas, any arrangement — "2 slices of pepperoni, 10 of cheese, the rest Hawaiian" (2 + 10 + 4 = 16).
  • Fractions of the order (from level 15): the counts are phrased as fractions and the child converts them — "an eighth pepperoni, five eighths cheese, a quarter Hawaiian" = 2, 10 and 4 slices.
  • Fractions bigger than one pizza: a single kind can fill more than a whole pizza — "three quarters bbq" is 12 of 16, a whole pizza plus four more. Tapping a recipe name shows what is in it.

Position and shape Why: trains spatial vocabulary and orientation-independent thinking — orders use relations, not fixed directions.

  • Next to / touching: two slices that share an edge.
  • Opposite: straight across the pizza.
  • Diagonal: two quarters corner to corner.
  • Every other: skip one each time, all the way round.
  • Three in a row: a line of touching slices.

Number and comparison Why: puts "how many" into words — exact counts, greater-than / less-than, and "at least one".

  • Exactly N: "exactly one fruit on every slice."
  • More than / fewer than: "more than two meats" = 3, 4, 5…; "fewer than five" = 4 or below.
  • At least one: "at least one meat" — one is fine, more is fine, never zero.

Sets and categories Why: classification and set membership — grouping things, and the idea one thing can be in two groups at once (the basis of Venn diagrams).

  • Meat: pepperoni, ham, bacon, sausage, meatball, chicken.
  • Vegetable: mushroom, pepper, onion, olive, spinach, sweetcorn.
  • Fruit: pineapple, banana, raisins.
  • Silly topping: broccoli, banana, marshmallow, fish heads and friends.
  • Belonging to two groups: broccoli is a vegetable and silly; banana is a fruit and silly.
  • Intersection: in both groups at once — spatial ("in both the top half and the right half") and by category ("both a fruit AND silly").
  • Shared property: group by a trait they share — "one green topping on every slice."
  • Not touching: keep two groups apart with a gap.

Logic and language Why: the heart of comprehension — following a reference back, evaluating a condition, ruling options out, inferring an unstated rule.

  • Except / without: do it all, leave one part out.
  • Coverage with an exception: "cover it all in banana, but one slice should not have banana."
  • Riddle: a topping named by a clue, not its word.
  • Pronouns (it, the other one): tracking what a word points back to.
  • Either / or: choose one of two options.
  • Not both: one or the other, never together.
  • Conditional: check whether something is true, then act on it.
  • Deduction by elimination: rule groups out to find what is left.
  • Inference: work out an unstated rule — "can't eat meat" → no meat.
  • Named pizza (recipe): a name standing for a fixed set of toppings.
  • Base: the sauce laid first (tomato, cheese or BBQ).
  • Red herrings & self-correction: chatter to ignore, and orders that change their mind.

Rules and instruction words Why: children are constantly told to do things with words of different force. Knowing must ≠ may ≠ must not is the difference between following instructions exactly and roughly.

  • Must / shall / required: you have to do it.
  • May / optional / should / recommended: your choice — doing it and skipping it are both fine.
  • Must not / shall not: never do it.
  • The game accepts the required parts, allows the optional ones either way, and marks the forbidden ones wrong.

"Vegetarian" is taught as a word (a pizza with no meat) rather than a graded rule: the game checks the slices, not the label.

Under the hood: early Boolean logic

Several of the top-level constructs are the exact building blocks of Boolean algebra — the binary logic all computer arithmetic is built from. It is genuine computational thinking, dressed up as pizza, years before a child meets a truth table.

  • AND: "both ham AND mushroom," or "a fruit AND silly" (the intersection of two sets).
  • OR: "either ham or pineapple" — at least one is true.
  • NOT: "no meat," "must not," "one slice should not have banana."
  • Exclusive OR (XOR): "one or the other, but not both."
  • True / false: "is pepperoni a meat? Yes" — evaluating a condition before acting.
A nice follow-up conversation: ask which orders are secretly an AND, an OR, or a NOT. The pizza makes the abstract idea concrete.
